# Podcast Discovery Research Report
**3 Participants | February 2026**

---

## 1. LISTENING CONTEXT

### Platform migration to YouTube is underway
**Evidence:**
- P3: "I definitely consume a lot more podcast material on YouTube... not on Spotify"
- P3 initially reported listening "once a month" but consumes podcast content daily on YouTube
- P2 burns out quickly on audio-only shows but watches YouTube podcast clips regularly

**Recommendation:** Any podcast product must integrate with or acknowledge YouTube's video podcast ecosystem. Audio-only apps are losing users to video platforms.

### Work-from-home eliminated commute listening time
**Evidence:**
- P3: "When I commuted a lot more, I was listening to podcasts almost every day. Versus working from home, not as much"
- All participants now listen during chores (cooking, cleaning, laundry) instead of commuting
- P2 uses AirPods while cooking because kitchen noise drowns out speakers

**Recommendation:** Design for at-home multitasking contexts. Physical devices need Bluetooth for headphone compatibility, not just speakers.

---

## 2. DISCOVERY

### Discovery methods split three ways: network chains, platform-passive, and algorithm-active
**Evidence:**
- P1: "It's just like a chain" — follows hosts to new shows, discovers through guest appearances
- P2: Relies on Spotify interface, doesn't actively search, wishes platform would surface better content
- P3: YouTube algorithm responds to clicked topics, autoplay continues until manual skip

**Recommendation:** Support all three discovery modes. Network users need guest/host cross-referencing. Passive users need smart curation. Active users need search and metadata filtering.

### Life events trigger deep content dives
**Evidence:**
- P3: Relationship issues → relationship podcast rabbit hole; new job → financial content surge
- P2: Work involves politics → burnt out on The Daily, wants deeper contextual content not daily news
- P1: Phases with historical podcasts, crime podcasts, Cambodian language content for cultural connection

**Recommendation:** Detect deep-dive patterns (multiple episodes in same topic area over short time) and surface related content automatically.

### Current platforms fail at format-based discovery
**Evidence:**
- P2: "I don't know how to currently search for serial podcasts" on Spotify
- P1: Stopped listening when favorite show switched from serialized to episodic format
- P3: Would want serialized content for road trips but no way to filter

**Recommendation:** Make serialized vs. episodic a first-class filter. Format significantly affects listening decisions.

---

## 3. EVALUATION & SELECTION

### Users assess quality within first 10 minutes (or less)
**Evidence:**
- P3: "The first 10 minutes, I'll kind of know if they're speaking to me"
- P2: "Usually within a few minutes, I can tell whether that's working for me or not"
- P1: Listens to 1-2 episodes before deciding

**Recommendation:** Surface quality signals upfront (production value indicators, user ratings from similar listeners) to reduce wasted trial time.

### Content must match current knowledge level
**Evidence:**
- P3: "I don't want it to be so simple... but I'm also not day trading" — needs content between basics and expert level
- P3: First 10 minutes answers: "Am I going to get anything out of this or is this over my head or is this way too easy?"

**Recommendation:** Tag content by knowledge level (beginner/intermediate/advanced) and learn user's level through behavior patterns.

### Production quality trumps topic relevance
**Evidence:**
- P2 (journalism background): "I would rather listen to one that's pretty well thought out" — prioritizes high production
- P2: On local content concept: "I could be convinced to be interested in most topics" IF production quality is high
- P3: Rejects hosts who are "full of shit" or too "over the top"

**Recommendation:** Build quality filters and signals. Show production indicators, evidence-based tags, or creator credentials.

### Metadata drives pre-consumption decisions
**Evidence:**
- P3's three criteria: view count (popularity), publication date (recency), length (time commitment)
- P3: "YouTube likes to suggest videos that were 2 years ago. I'm like, I don't care about that"
- All participants check episode length before starting

**Recommendation:** Make view count, publication date, and length prominent. Add recency filters (last 4 days, last week, last month). Relevance matters more for news/tech than evergreen topics.

---

## 4. LISTENING EXPERIENCE

### Attention level determines content selection
**Evidence:**
- P2: Heavyweight requires full attention "like an audiobook," but Two Dykes and a Mic works while driving in city
- P1: Filters by format and topic based on activity
- P3: Travel = entertainment (Sasquatch, UFOs), home = educational (finance, relationships)

**Recommendation:** Tag content by attention level required. Surface low-attention content for multitasking, high-attention content for focused listening.

### Users check episode descriptions during/after listening
**Evidence:**
- P1: "Usually in the description" — references it when hosts mention specific details like restaurant names
- P1: Checks descriptions during, before, and after listening

**Recommendation:** Physical devices need way to access episode descriptions. Consider companion app or display descriptions on device screen.

---

## 5. BURN OUT & RETENTION

### Users burn out from format familiarity, not content
**Evidence:**
- P2: "Tone and arc... the people are too familiar" — burnt out on This American Life and Heavyweight despite loving them
- P2: "Ira Glass is such a distinctive [voice]" — can predict storytelling, loses element of surprise
- P3: Gets fixated on podcast, listens heavily for weeks, then abandons

**Recommendation:** When engagement with a show declines, recommend shows with similar topics but different formats/hosts. Break familiarity patterns proactively.

---

## 6. CONCEPT TESTING RESULTS

### Three features had universal appeal
**Evidence:**
- All participants liked: (1) format-based filtering (serialized vs. episodic), (2) current events in local area, (3) conversational vs. structured style
- P3: "Those 3 for sure" when asked which features would help most
- P1 wanted "some mixture of all of these"

**Recommendation:** Prioritize these three filters in any product. They address real pain points across all user types.

### Product preference split by user archetype
**Evidence:**
- P1: Physical desk radio preferred (wants customization via settings)
- P2: App version "most practical" — won't purchase separate device
- P3: Likes handheld aesthetically but questions functionality; app needed for actual use

**Recommendation:** Build app first (serves P2 and P3). Physical devices are niche/premium products for specific contexts (P1's desk setup, bedside use).

### App differentiation requires more than new categories
**Evidence:**
- P1: App concept "doesn't really feel any different to me" than Apple Podcasts
- P1: "It doesn't really show me anything new. It looks like Apple"
- P1: "Experience of using it" must feel different, not just content organization

**Recommendation:** Apps need interaction model differentiation, not just better categorization. Consider radio metaphor, auto-progression, or other novel interactions.

### Physical devices need companion apps for settings
**Evidence:**
- All participants questioned how to customize physical device
- P1: "Probably somewhere else, like on an app" for customization
- P3: "Set your criteria somehow... via an app on your phone"

**Recommendation:** Physical devices cannot be fully self-contained for settings. Require companion app for preference management while maintaining simple device interface.

---

## 7. SMS CONFIGURATION FEASIBILITY

### Users would accept SMS for device configuration
**Evidence:**
- P1: Currently checks episode descriptions on phone while listening on device
- All participants already use phones alongside listening devices (checking info, queuing)
- Pattern of "device for playback, phone for management" already established

**Recommendation:** SMS configuration is feasible for physical device settings. Use for infrequent tasks (setup, preference changes) not frequent tasks (queuing, search).

---

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **YouTube is the new podcast platform** — video podcasts dominate consumption for younger demographics
2. **Format filtering is critical gap** — users want serialized vs. episodic but can't find it anywhere
3. **Quality over topic** — production value and appropriate complexity level matter more than subject relevance
4. **Three user archetypes** — network-driven (P1), platform-passive (P2), algorithm-active (P3) — require different discovery approaches
5. **Apps need novel interactions** — better categories alone insufficient; must feel different to use
6. **Physical devices are niche** — companion app required; works for specific contexts (desk, bedside) not primary listening
